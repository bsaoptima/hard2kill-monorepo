# What Is Hard2Kill? Platform Identity

> The positioning that wins: **Hard2Kill is the first skill-based prediction market.**

---

## The One-Liner

**Polymarket lets you bet on elections. We let you bet on yourself.**

Hard2Kill is a new category — not a casino, not a tournament platform, not a prediction market for external events. It's a real-time prediction market where the market IS you. Your skill. Your performance. Your ability to beat another human in competition.

Two players each put money on a single belief: *"I will win this match."* They play. The server determines who was right. Money moves instantly.

That's a prediction market. The outcome is a belief about skill, not chance. The counter-party is another human making the opposite bet. The settlement is automatic and cheat-proof.

We share the **legal structure** of skill gaming (which is why we don't need a gambling license in 36+ US states). We share the **user psychology** of prediction markets (put money where your mouth is). We share the **product dynamics** of real-time PvP gaming (ego, competition, rivalry).

But we are none of them. We are a new category.

## Why This Is a New Category — Not "Players' Lounge But Better"

It would be easy to position Hard2Kill as "a better version of Players' Lounge / Skillz / Repeat.gg." Easy, and wrong. Here's why:

### The skill-gaming category ceiling is $100-500M/year

- Skillz peaked at $400M GMV and is now declining ($95M revenue, 99.6% stock decline)
- Players' Lounge: $7.8M/year, $54M valuation after 10 years and $18M raised
- Repeat.gg: acquired by Sony for an undisclosed (modest) figure

The "skill-gaming platform" category is a grinder. Incumbents take 10 years to build $10M businesses. Valuations are modest. Exits are acqui-hires.

### The prediction market category is ascendant

- Polymarket: $9B+ volume in 2024, rumored $1B valuation
- Kalshi: raised at $2B valuation in 2025
- DraftKings: $15B market cap
- Online gambling: $121B global

These are different magnitude businesses. The prediction-market framing unlocks the narrative, the multiples, and the investor base that the skill-gaming framing doesn't.

### The product IS a prediction market, we just haven't been framing it that way

Look at what actually happens in a Hard2Kill match:
- Two participants each express a belief ("I will win") by committing money
- A counter-party takes the opposite position
- An oracle (our server) resolves the outcome
- Winners are paid out based on the resolution

That's the literal definition of a binary prediction market. The only difference from Polymarket is: **the event being priced is your own skill, not an external event.**

That's not a lesser version of a prediction market. That's a completely new branch of one.

## What Stays The Same Legally

Positioning is branding and narrative. Legally, we're still a skill-based gaming platform. This is critical.

**The gambling test:** Gambling requires three elements — prize, paid entry, outcome determined by chance. Hard2Kill satisfies prize and entry, but NOT chance. Match outcomes are determined by player skill (aim, timing, reads, execution). No RNG, no random damage, no loot draws. This makes us a game of skill under the dominant US legal test.

**Skillz's precedent:** Their patented "predominance test" requires a skilled player to beat an unskilled player 75%+ of the time. Our games (server-authoritative, no RNG) clear this bar easily.

**What this means:**
- No gambling license needed in 36+ US states
- No CFTC approval needed (we're self-referential — you bet on yourself, not on an external event that would trigger event-market regulation)
- Operate globally in most markets without the regulatory hell that casinos and traditional prediction markets face
- Same legal framework that Skillz operated under at $16B peak valuation

**Restricted states** (skill-gaming blocked): AZ, AR, CT, DE, FL, IN, LA, MD, MN, MT, SC, SD, TN, WY. Block these — same 14 that Players' Lounge blocks.

## The Positioning That Wins

### What to say

- **Top-of-funnel / press / investors:** "The first skill-based prediction market."
- **Casual users:** "Bet on yourself. Win on skill."
- **Competitive gamers / FGC:** "Automated money matches. Server picks the winner. Instant payouts."
- **Comparison framing:** "Polymarket for your own gameplay."
- **Regulators:** "Skill-based competitive gaming. No element of chance."

### What NOT to say

- **Gambling, betting, casino** — legal and platform risk, and wrong category
- **Prediction market** (unqualified) — triggers CFTC framing, not accurate to what we are
- **Tournament platform** — diminishes us to Skillz/PL territory
- **Esports** — we're not esports, we're money matches (different culture, different audience)
- **Sportsbook** — we're not betting on other people's performances

### The brand identity

The emotional core of Hard2Kill is **ego + conviction**:
- *"I'm better than you and I'll prove it with $50."*
- Not luck. Not dopamine. Not "maybe this time."
- **Skill, self-belief, and the willingness to put money on it.**

This is fundamentally different from casino psychology (hope + variance) and fundamentally different from traditional prediction market psychology (analysis + information edge). It's closer to poker's ego economy, closer to the FGC's money-match culture, closer to the "pick-up basketball for cash" vibe that has existed forever.

We're productizing an ancient human impulse: *"I'll bet you I can beat you."*

## The Rake: Our House Edge

Every sustainable wagering business takes a cut:

- Online casinos: 2-15% house edge on chance games
- Poker rooms: 2.5-10% rake (capped)
- Prediction markets: 1-5% fees
- Sports books: ~5% vig
- Players' Lounge: 10% rake
- Skillz: takes a cut of every entry fee

**Hard2Kill: 10% rake on every cash match** — standard for PvP wagering.

Player A and Player B each wager $10. Winner receives $18. Platform receives $2.

This is the business. It scales linearly with match volume (just like a casino scales with bet frequency). Poker proved this is a $1B+ category. The rake doesn't need to apologize for itself.

**When to turn it on:** at 50+ DAU, when users are habituated to the platform. Frame transparently: *"We're adding a 5% platform fee to keep servers running and fund bigger tournament prize pools."*
